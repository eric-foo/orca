## 5. First-Order Evidence Units

### FO-1 - CoCounsel Was Public And Task-Specific Before Cutoff

Casetext publicly launched CoCounsel on 2023-03-01 as a legal AI assistant using OpenAI technology. The launch framed concrete legal workflows: legal research, document review, deposition preparation, contract analysis, and lawyer-facing task delegation. It also positioned CoCounsel as legal-specific, secure, and reliable rather than a general public chatbot.

Signal effect: positive capability signal, but still vendor-controlled. It proves public product visibility and strategic relevance, not durable buyer pull or acquisition value.

### FO-2 - Thomson Reuters Had A Pre-Cutoff Generative-AI Legal Strategy

In the 2023-05-02 earnings call transcript, Thomson Reuters described generative AI as applicable across legal, tax, and risk markets; tied it to legal research and workflow software; cited Westlaw, Practical Law, proprietary content, and accuracy as strategic advantages; and said it planned material ongoing AI investment and generative-AI capabilities in Westlaw Precision and Practical Law in the second half of 2023.

Signal effect: strong incumbent-strategy fit. It supports a build, partner, license, or capability-capture path more than passive watch. It does not, by itself, select Casetext or prove acquisition.

### FO-3 - TR-Curated Law-Firm Survey Showed Interest But Low Adoption And High Risk

The Thomson Reuters Institute law-firm report was based on late-March 2023 respondents from large and midsize law firms. It showed broad belief that generative AI could apply to legal work and meaningful consideration by firms, while also showing very low current adoption and substantial concerns around accuracy, security, privacy, and confidentiality.

Signal effect: useful market-timing signal with caution. It raises the ceiling above generic watch, but it caps certainty because the buyer state was still consideration-heavy and risk-heavy.

## 6. Second-Order Evidence Units

### SO-1 - Public Law-Firm Deployment Showed Early Practitioner Pull

Fisher Phillips publicly described deployment of CoCounsel and named legal research, document review, contract revision, drafting, and summarization as intended firm workflows. This was a stronger signal than vendor launch copy because it came from a law firm and described real workflow insertion. It remained customer/partner-shaped and was not broad-market proof.

Signal effect: upgrade from pure test curiosity toward credible capability capture. It supports partner, license, build-with-target, or acquisition diligence, not unconditional acquisition.

### SO-2 - Court Sanctions Made Professional Risk Concrete Before Cutoff

The 2023-06-22 SDNY sanctions order and contemporaneous legal reporting made hallucinated legal authority and lawyer gatekeeping a live professional-risk issue before cutoff. The court source did not reject AI categorically, but it made verification, accuracy, and professional responsibility unavoidable.

Signal effect: reframe. Legal AI action should be anchored in verified legal content, workflow controls, and professional-grade safeguards. This downgrades hype-driven `move` while strengthening the case for an incumbent with authoritative content and legal workflow distribution.

### SO-3 - Broad Analyst Evidence Supported Enterprise Workflow Timing, Not Target Selection

Goldman Sachs' 2023-04-05 AI analysis showed that generative AI was already viewed as a potentially large software and workflow shift, with monetization through AI-integrated offerings and workflow automation. The source is broad, not legal-specific, so it supports urgency but not a reliable legal acquisition decision on its own.

Signal effect: modest upgrade on timing pressure; no proof of legal buyer willingness to pay or Casetext-specific moat.

## 8. At-Cutoff Decision Memo

- Decision frame: As of 2023-06-25, Thomson Reuters should decide whether legal AI assistants such as CoCounsel require passive monitoring, narrow testing, partnership/licensing, internal build, acquisition diligence, or a bounded move.
- Evidence basis: CoCounsel was publicly launched as a legal-task assistant; Thomson Reuters had a public AI strategy tied to Westlaw, Practical Law, proprietary content, and workflow software; law-firm and survey evidence showed early practitioner interest and consideration; court-risk evidence made accuracy and professional controls material.
- Signal Integrity: strong on public visibility and strategic fit; medium on practitioner workflow salience; weak on broad buyer adoption, willingness to pay, target valuation, and integration feasibility.
- Signal Use Classification: `decision-shaping evidence`, not `reliable-bet proof`.
- First-order versus second-order movement: first-order evidence supports `test/build/license/partner diligence`; second-order evidence upgrades urgency and reframes the correct action toward professional-grade capability capture with risk controls.
- Decision Strength: `MODERATE_POSITIVE_ACTION_SIGNAL`.
- Action Ceiling: `BOUNDED_MOVE_CAPABILITY_CAPTURE_WITH_DILIGENCE`.
- Recommendation: `move` only as a bounded capability-capture program: run immediate acquisition/partner/license diligence on CoCounsel-class capability; accelerate Westlaw and Practical Law generative-AI build paths; test narrowly in legal research, drafting, document review, and contract workflows; require accuracy, confidentiality, citation-verification, and professional-responsibility gates before broad launch.
- Counterevidence: current law-firm adoption was low; many firms were still only considering use; risk concerns were substantial; the strongest CoCounsel evidence was vendor/customer-shaped; public data did not establish pricing power, retention lift, target valuation, or integration success.
- Alternative explanations: practitioner interest may reflect temporary ChatGPT attention; law-firm deployment may be a narrow innovation-led pilot; TR's own content and workflow assets may support build without acquisition; professional risk may slow adoption despite technical promise.
- Uncertainty: buyer urgency, procurement readiness, enterprise governance, target defensibility, model reliability, integration cost, and actual usage depth were not publicly proven.
- Kill criteria: broad firm adoption remains negligible after controlled pilots; legal hallucination and confidentiality controls fail; CoCounsel-class output cannot be tied to authoritative content and verification; acquisition valuation requires adoption assumptions not visible in customer behavior; build/partner paths can close the capability gap faster with less risk.
- Update triggers: repeated independent law-firm deployments; procurement-ready enterprise controls; demonstrable citation verification; sustained legal workflow usage; competitor legal-AI integrations; public evidence that customers prefer integrated incumbent workflow over standalone tools.
- Boundary note: do not use the later acquisition outcome to raise the at-cutoff ceiling. Public evidence supports bounded capability capture and diligence, not a proven acquisition mandate.
