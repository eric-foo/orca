## Report (as provided)

# Manufactured and artificially amplified demand in beauty and personal care

*Evidence reviewed through June 19, 2026.*

## Executive summary

- **Is it real? — Yes (High confidence).** Regulators have documented employee-written beauty
  reviews, employees presented as ordinary shoppers, hidden paid or affiliate promotion, and
  commercial markets for fake followers and engagement. A peer-reviewed Amazon study directly
  observed sellers recruiting reimbursed five-star reviewers; Beauty & Personal Care was the
  largest product category in its selected sample.
- **Is it prevalent? — Hidden commercial amplification is plainly not rare in regulator sweeps,
  but the population prevalence of outright fake demand is unknown (Medium for hidden promotion;
  low for an exact fake-demand rate).** An ACCC targeted sweep found concerns in 12 of 16
  beauty/cosmetics accounts reviewed, while an EU cross-sector sweep found only 20% of 576
  influencers systematically disclosed commercial content. Both samples were selected for
  enforcement screening and are not representative market surveys.
- **Can it be detected publicly? — Often as a risk signal, rarely as proof from one metric
  (Medium; high only with direct or convergent evidence).** Reviewer-network overlap, direct
  broker solicitations, synchronized activity, disclosure gaps, post-campaign decay and
  cross-platform inconsistencies are useful. Product–reviewer network features have outperformed
  text-only features in research using directly observed fake-review buyers.
- **Does demand collapse when amplification stops? — Sometimes (High for that cohort; unknown for
  named brands).** In the Amazon cohort, rating, review and sales gains were temporary and moved
  back toward pre-campaign levels; subsequent one-star review share increased. For the named
  beauty enforcement cases, public sales, retention and repeat-purchase data generally do not
  exist, so collapse cannot responsibly be claimed.

**Core conclusion:** manufactured demand exists across the sector, but public evidence supports
classification by probability rather than accusation. A follower spike, sellout or review burst
alone is weak evidence. Confidence becomes materially stronger when several independent signals
converge — particularly direct solicitation evidence, shared-reviewer networks and failure of
real purchasing behavior to persist after promotion.

"Hidden promotion" should not be equated with "no real demand." It establishes that the origin of
attention was concealed, not that every buyer or favorable opinion was inauthentic.

## 1. Documented cases

- **Sunday Riley Modern Skincare — FTC complaint resolved by final consent order.** The FTC
  alleged that managers and employees posted Sephora reviews through false identities, were
  instructed to leave five stars, used a VPN after Sephora removed reviews associated with company
  IP addresses, and down-voted negative reviews. *Discovery / signals:* an anonymous former
  employee published an internal email and screenshots; the FTC subsequently quoted internal
  instructions in its complaint. The strongest evidence was a whistleblower document, corroborated
  by the regulator — not merely an unusual star-rating pattern. *Demand afterward:* the order
  prohibited future misrepresentation and required disclosure of employee connections. **No demand
  collapse is documented.** A dissenting FTC commissioner stated that little appeared to have
  changed commercially and that Sephora remained a major sales channel; an enforcement-policy
  observation, not a formal sales study.
- **Anonymized Amazon sellers, including 193 Beauty & Personal Care products — peer-reviewed
  direct-observation study.** Sellers recruited reviewers in Facebook groups, required a full-price
  Amazon purchase and five-star review, then reimbursed the purchase through PayPal, sometimes
  paying an additional commission. The resulting review could display as a "Verified Purchase."
  *Discovery / signals:* researchers directly observed recruitment posts and matched them to
  subsequent Amazon reviews, ratings, sales rank, price and advertising data. Beauty & Personal
  Care was the largest category among roughly 1,500 identified products; campaigns generated
  observable review and rating bursts. *Demand afterward:* **the amplification was temporary rather
  than durable** — sales and ratings improved during manipulation but moved back toward
  pre-campaign levels within weeks; average ratings subsequently declined and the one-star share
  increased. The strongest direct evidence here of amplified-but-hollow demand, though it describes
  an intentionally selected fake-review cohort rather than all beauty products.
- **Hismile — ACCC infringement notices and court-enforceable undertaking, June 2026.** Social
  videos presented apparently random shoppers praising Hismile products; the people shown were
  Hismile employees. Separate Glostik Tooth Gloss videos allegedly implied stain removal even
  though the product temporarily concealed stains. *Discovery / signals:* the decisive signal was
  identity verification — matching the supposed members of the public to company employment.
  Product demonstrations could also be compared with the product's actual mechanism. Hismile
  admitted the conduct was, or was likely, misleading under Australian Consumer Law. *Demand
  afterward:* Hismile paid AUD 138,600, accepted a compliance program and agreed not to represent
  employees as random reviewers. Glostik was discontinued, but the record does not establish that
  enforcement or weak demand caused the discontinuation. Company-wide demand effects are unknown.
- **Teami — FTC allegations resolved by stipulated settlement.** Teami, which marketed teas and
  skincare, allegedly used hundreds of paid influencer posts with disclosures that were absent,
  hidden below Instagram's "more" cut or otherwise inadequate. The case also involved unsupported
  health claims, mainly concerning teas. *Signals:* repeated product endorsements, known paid
  relationships and disclosures not visible before a user expanded the caption; conduct continued
  after an FTC warning. *Demand afterward:* a USD 15.2 million judgment, largely suspended after a
  USD 1 million payment. Public records do not isolate skincare sales, repeat purchase or demand
  decay, so no collapse conclusion is supportable.
- **Charlotte Tilbury Beauty and Eliza Batten — upheld ASA ruling.** Instagram content featured
  gifted cosmetics and swipe-up affiliate links that generated commission, but used "#collab"
  rather than a clear, upfront advertising disclosure. *Signal:* a direct purchase link tied to
  affiliate compensation, combined with no prominent "Ad" label before exposure. *Demand
  afterward:* future affiliate advertising ordered to be clearly identified. No public sales or
  repeat-purchase data — documents hidden amplification, **not proof that underlying demand was
  hollow.**
- **THG Beauty/Lookfantastic, The Fragrance Shop and Erim Kaur — upheld ASA ruling.** A fragrance
  story promoted "4 long-lasting perfumes" through an LTK affiliate link; "Paid links" appeared
  only after users clicked through. *Demand afterward:* ordered not to reappear in that form; no
  product-level sales/repeat data; hidden affiliate origin does not establish fake fragrance
  demand.
- **BPerfect Cosmetics and Charlotte Dawson — upheld ASA ruling.** Affiliate and discount-code
  stories had an advertising label rendered white over white clothing; a beauty filter ("Luxurious")
  altered the complexion relevant to the promoted makeup's apparent result. *Demand afterward:* ads
  prohibited in the investigated form; no public demand/retention effect — concealed and visually
  enhanced promotion, not fabricated purchasers.
- **Devumi — FTC court settlement; enabling infrastructure, not a beauty-brand finding.** The FTC
  alleged Devumi sold fake followers, subscribers, views and likes (reportedly 58,000+
  fake-Twitter-follower orders, among others). **No beauty client should be inferred.** *Demand
  afterward:* Devumi already defunct; USD 2.5 million judgment, suspended after USD 250,000. No
  category-level consumer-demand effect measured.

**What these cases do — and do not — establish:** they establish that fake reviews, fake public
testimonials, undisclosed affiliate promotion and purchased social indicators are real mechanisms.
Only the Amazon research measures the subsequent trajectory of product sales and reviews. The
enforcement record is much stronger on **whether a representation was deceptive** than on **whether
total consumer demand disappeared once exposed**.

## 2. Detection methods and public tells

*Reliability below concerns whether a signal justifies escalating scrutiny. Except for direct
documentary or transaction evidence, no individual tell proves manipulation.*

- **Review timing bursts.** A new/quiet SKU receives an unusually dense cluster of reviews
  (often mostly five-star) over a short period; volume then stops abruptly. *Reliability:* **Medium
  alone; high when aligned with recruitment posts, shared reviewers or identical language.**
  Legitimate launches, sampling and retailer migrations also produce bursts. *Evaded by:*
  drip-feeding over weeks, time-zone randomization, mixing with ordinary ratings.
- **Duplicated or templated language.** Exact/near-exact phrases recur across reviewers or
  unrelated products; identical claim order, generic superlatives, details inconsistent with the
  SKU. *Reliability:* **High for exact duplicates; low-to-medium for generic enthusiasm.**
  Text-only classifiers can perform poorly out of training domain. *Evaded by:* paraphrasing,
  translation, generative AI; real sampling campaigns may independently prompt the same talking
  points.
- **Reviewer-history anomalies.** Many reviewers have very new accounts, little history, extreme
  rating distributions, or review many unrelated products rapidly; or an experienced account
  repeatedly reviews obscure, unrelated products. *Reliability:* **Medium** (stronger as a cohort
  statistic than about one reviewer). *Evaded by:* aged accounts, varied ratings, inserted ordinary
  purchases, recruiting established reviewers.
- **Shared-reviewer or co-review graph.** The same relatively small pool of reviewers repeatedly
  appears across products from otherwise unrelated sellers; dense clusters, high centrality,
  repeated overlap. *Reliability:* **High — among the strongest available public-data signals.** In
  direct-ground-truth research, **network features outperformed review text, image and standard
  metadata features.** *Evaded by:* partitioning reviewer pools or recruiting more unique
  participants (raises cost, reduces scale); limited public access to reviewer histories can block
  analysis.
- **"Verified purchase" plus reimbursement pattern.** Reviews display a verified-purchase badge,
  but matching off-platform posts offer reimbursement after a five-star review. *Reliability:*
  **High when the same product is found in a reimbursement group; low if relying on the badge
  alone.** *Evaded by:* reimbursements moving to private messages/encrypted services/intermediaries;
  obscured product identifiers.
- **Synchronized creator or account activity.** Multiple accounts post the same/similar text, URL,
  image, hashtag set or claim within a narrow window. *Reliability:* **Medium** — reliably detects
  coordination but not its legitimacy (PR embargoes, launches and disclosed campaigns are
  coordinated too). *Evaded by:* varying wording/media, staggering times, rotating links, mixing
  paid creators with ordinary-looking accounts.
- **Follower and engagement-graph anomalies.** Sudden follower jumps; repeated engagement from the
  same cluster; generic comments; audience geography/language inconsistent with content; views,
  likes and followers moving together in implausibly regular increments. *Reliability:*
  **Low-to-medium alone** (audits are proprietary estimates; legitimate virality, giveaways or
  international expansion can resemble manipulation). Direct purchase records or platform action are
  much stronger. *Evaded by:* engagement from real but low-intent humans, gradual purchases,
  demographically plausible targeting.
- **Undisclosed-promotion gap.** An affiliate URL, discount code, gifted product, brand-control
  relationship or repeated campaign claim appears without a clear advertising label at the start of
  the content. *Reliability:* **High for identifying concealed commercial amplification when the
  material connection is verifiable.** It does **not** show whether the creator genuinely liked the
  product or whether buyers repeat-purchased. *Evaded by:* ambiguous "collab," disclosures after
  engagement, temporary stories, affiliate redirects hiding the destination.
- **Engagement without conversion.** Social mentions/likes/views rise sharply, but branded search,
  retailer rank, review flow, retailer reorders or sustained sales do not follow; or conversion
  exists only during extreme discounting/reimbursement. *Reliability:* **Low with public social
  metrics alone; medium with independent sales-rank or retail data.** The Amazon study shows
  manipulated ratings can create real-but-temporary sales rather than zero sales. *Evaded/misread
  by:* channel shifts, stock constraints, retailer promotions, unavailable private sales data;
  paid media can produce awareness before conversion.
- **Post-campaign decay and cross-venue divergence.** Reviews/ratings jump on one platform during a
  campaign, then later ratings deteriorate or one-star reviews increase; other retailers or
  communities report materially different experiences. *Reliability:* **Medium-to-high when the
  break aligns with campaign start/end and appears in multiple measures.** *Evaded by:* prolonging
  incentives, switching listings, merging variants, moving retailer, discounting to sustain nominal
  unit sales.
- **Costly-behavior persistence.** Full-price sell-through continues after campaign support;
  organic review volume persists; retailers reorder or expand distribution; replenishable products
  generate repeat purchasing; consumers switch from competing products without continuing
  incentives. *Reliability:* **Best separator of hollow from genuine demand, but often unavailable
  publicly.** A sellout alone is weak (low initial inventory manufactures scarcity); multiple
  sustained indicators are more persuasive than any single one. *Evaded by:* shortages, discounts,
  bundles, channel inventory, repeated paid bursts to simulate momentum; outsiders usually cannot
  observe cohort retention, returns or acquisition payback directly.

**Practical evidentiary hierarchy:**
1. **Highest confidence:** internal documents, whistleblowers, payment or reimbursement records,
   broker advertisements matched to products, regulator findings and platform enforcement.
2. **Strong public-data evidence:** dense shared-reviewer networks plus timing and text anomalies.
3. **Moderate evidence:** synchronized posting, disclosure gaps and post-campaign deterioration.
4. **Weak alone:** follower count, engagement rate, a viral hashtag, a sellout, a five-star average
   or a single suspicious review.

## 3. The three-way distinction

- **A. Outright fake.** *Economic reality:* bots, fabricated identities, employee reviews or
  purchased reviews create social proof without independent consumer preference; some reimbursed
  "purchases" occur but carry little economic cost. *Public signature:* direct broker/reimbursement
  evidence; impossible or highly concentrated reviewer histories; dense shared-reviewer clusters;
  exact duplicates; platform removals. *When support stops:* counts may be deleted; little durable
  sales/organic activity remains unless manipulation recruited later real buyers.
- **B. Amplified-but-hollow.** *Economic reality:* real advertising, seeding, discounts or
  reimbursed purchases produce attention and some genuine transactions, but satisfaction, repeat
  purchase or willingness to pay is weak. *Public signature:* campaign-period lift then fast decay;
  discount dependence; rising later negative reviews; limited retailer reorder; social engagement
  materially exceeds durable purchasing. The Amazon cohort is the clearest documented example.
  *When support stops:* sales/search/review growth revert toward baseline; decline may be gradual
  rather than a complete collapse.
- **C. Genuine demand with normal amplification.** *Economic reality:* paid media, gifting and
  creator seeding accelerate awareness, but independent consumers subsequently pay, return,
  recommend and repurchase; material connections are disclosed. *Public signature:* sustained
  full-price sales after campaigns; organic review inflow; repeat/replenishment; retailer reorders
  and broader distribution; reasonably consistent cross-venue sentiment. *When support stops:*
  demand persists at a commercially meaningful level.

**Separating B from C.** The decisive question is not "Was the launch marketed?" — nearly every
serious launch is. It is whether **costly behavior persists** after marketing pressure falls:
*persistence* (sales/search/unsolicited reviews stay elevated after the campaign window); *price
integrity* (demand survives removal of reimbursement, deep discounts, constant bundles); *repeat
behavior* (replenishable skincare/fragrance/personal-care generate credible repurchase or reorder
signals); *cross-venue confirmation* (retailer ratings, independent communities and later
purchasers corroborate launch-period claims). **A practical research rule is to require at least
two independent persistence signals before classifying heavily promoted demand as durable. This is
an analytical safeguard, not a validated statistical cutoff.**

## 4. Prevalence and base rate

**What has actually been measured (all samples selection-limited):**
- **ACCC 2023 influencer sweep:** reviewed 118 influencers, concerns for 81% overall; in beauty and
  cosmetics, 12 of 16 accounts (75%) had concerning posts (commonly inadequately disclosed gifted/
  sponsored products). *Cannot support* a 75% prevalence estimate for all beauty influencers (sweep
  partly tip-off-informed; "concerns" not final legal findings).
- **EU 2023 influencer sweep:** 576 influencers across 22 member states; 97% published commercial
  content, only 20% systematically identified it, 62% earmarked for further investigation. *Cannot
  support* a beauty-specific rate or a finding that 62% violated law.
- **ASA 2024 monitoring:** automated analysis of 50,000+ pieces from 509 accounts (390
  influencers); 57% compliant, 9% inadequate disclosure, 34% no disclosure (mixed flagged/reported/
  random sample). *Cannot support* a pure random-sample population estimate or beauty-only rate.
- **ASA 2021 monitoring:** 122 UK accounts followed disclosure rules only 35% of the time (already
  within a compliance-monitoring exercise). *Cannot support* a general prevalence estimate.
- **Amazon fake-review research:** ~1,500 products advertised in fake-review groups; 193 Beauty &
  Personal Care (largest category). *Cannot support* the share of all Amazon beauty products buying
  fake reviews (selection began inside fake-review groups → no population denominator).
- **Amazon platform reporting:** blocked 250M+ suspected fake reviews in 2023; legal action against
  150+ alleged bad actors. *Cannot support* a prevalence rate (no submitted-review denominator,
  threshold, or independent audit).
- **EU online-review sweep:** 223 major websites checked; could not confirm adequate authenticity
  safeguards on 144; ≥55% potentially non-compliant. *Cannot support* the percentage of individual
  reviews that were fake or any beauty-specific rate.

**Segment conclusions:** Indie/DTC vs mass — **comparative prevalence unknown** (Amazon evidence is
third-party-marketplace-dominated, but "third-party seller" ≠ indie/DTC). Skincare — existence
firmly documented (Sunday Riley, Teami, Amazon cohort); no representative rate. Makeup/cosmetics —
hidden affiliate promotion and enhanced demonstrations documented; high concern in a small targeted
subsample; outright-fake prevalence unknown. Fragrance — undisclosed affiliate amplification
documented (THG/Fragrance Shop); no fragrance-specific base rate found. Personal/oral care —
Hismile shows staffed testimonials; category prevalence unknown. Mass market — public sales data may
ease durability evaluation, but no sound estimate that mass brands manipulate more/less than indie.

**Bottom line on prevalence:** available numbers measure selected accounts, selected websites,
suspected content or products already found in manipulation groups. **None supplies a defensible
percentage of total beauty demand that is fake, hollow or normally amplified.**

## 5. Tools, services and relevant research

- **FTC Consumer Reviews and Testimonials Rule** (effective Oct 21, 2024): civil penalties for
  knowing violations — fake reviews, sentiment-conditioned review incentives, undisclosed insider
  reviews, review suppression, fake independent review sites, fake social indicators. *Limit:* a
  legal framework, not a public detection engine; ordinary incentivized reviews are not
  automatically prohibited (conditioning compensation on sentiment is the key distinction).
- **FTC platform guidance:** recommends transparent collection/moderation/publication; neither open
  nor verified/closed systems are immune. *Limit:* no public scoring model.
- **Amazon internal detection + broker litigation:** ML + investigators; blocks suspected reviews;
  actions vs brokers (AppSally, Rebatest); broker page describes off-platform payments, coupons,
  reimbursements, automation, bots. *Limit:* company-controlled, opaque, not independently audited.
- **Bazaarvoice authenticity services:** language patterns, submission velocity, geography,
  consumer/submission characteristics across its network. *Limit:* proprietary, brand/retailer-only,
  not publicly reproducible.
- **PowerReviews review-integrity services:** IP monitoring, device reputation, duplicate
  identification for hosted programs. *Limit:* proprietary; depends on first-party metadata
  unavailable to public researchers.
- **HypeAuditor and Modash:** creator-audience audits (follower quality, growth, engagement, comment
  patterns). *Limit:* screening, not proof; proprietary definitions, incomplete platform data.
- **Coordination Network Toolkit / CooRTweet:** open-source co-posting/co-linking/text-similarity/
  short-window synchronization analysis. *Limit:* needs account-level posts + timestamps; detects
  coordination (deceptive or legitimate).
- **Botometer X:** archival bot scores for some X accounts created before June 2023. *Limit:* not
  suitable for current comprehensive auditing (incomplete coverage; historical model; not designed
  for modern generative-AI bots).
- **Fakespot:** former consumer review-analysis service. *Limit:* Mozilla shut down its website,
  extensions and apps on July 1, 2025 — not a current tool.
- **TikTok integrity enforcement:** prohibits automated operation, engagement manipulation, sales of
  fake followers/likes; reported removing 94%+ of fake-engagement-violating videos proactively in
  Q2 2024. *Limit:* platform-reported, all-category; no beauty-specific prevalence or thresholds.

**Research most useful for an analyst:** He, Hollenbeck & Proserpio (*The Market for Fake Reviews* —
direct fake-review-market observation linking recruitment to ratings/reviews/sales); He et al.
(*Detecting fake review buyers using network structure* — reviewer-network features beat text and
are costlier to evade); Fei et al. (review burstiness as a spam signal); Rayana & Akoglu (SpEagle —
collective text+metadata+network); Pacheco et al. (coordination detection).

## Final assessment

**Manufactured demand in beauty, skincare, fragrance and personal care is demonstrably real**
(regulator actions, internal instructions, admissions, affiliate records, direct fake-review-market
observation). **Its exact prevalence is not known** (no representative category-wide estimate; no
sound indie/DTC-vs-mass comparison). **It is partly detectable from public signals:** High with
direct documents/reimbursements/broker records/identity matching/platform-or-regulator confirmation;
Medium when reviewer networks, timing, text, coordination and post-campaign sales behavior converge;
Low on followers, engagement rates, star averages, virality or sellouts alone. **The most defensible
separator between hollow amplification and genuine amplified demand is sustained costly behavior:**
full-price purchases, repeat purchase, retailer reorders, stable cross-venue sentiment and sales
that persist after promotional support declines.
