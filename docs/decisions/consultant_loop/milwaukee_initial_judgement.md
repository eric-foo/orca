## Initial Judgement

Recommended action: adopt the newly authorized local revenue option at the full
available rate, while pairing adoption with a public fiscal-control package,
service-stability commitments, and a mitigation plan for the tax burden.

The reason is blunt: the pre-cutoff record does not show another recurring,
legally available tool large enough to cover the structural gap, rising pension
costs, expiring temporary federal relief, and already-reduced staffing base. The
state-law option is ugly because it uses a regressive tax instrument and carries
state-imposed constraints on local discretion. But rejecting it requires a
credible substitute of similar recurring scale. The clean pre-cutoff evidence
does not show one.

The recommended action ceiling is therefore: accept the revenue authority, but
do not treat the new revenue as a blank check. Use it only within a constrained
stabilization frame: pension solvency, core service preservation, public-safety
and emergency response obligations, infrastructure protection, and transparent
multi-year reporting. City leadership should avoid using the decision as a
mandate for broad new program expansion unless a later budget process can prove
the recurring money, legal permission, and service tradeoffs.

Confidence: 72%.

Biggest risk: the city stabilizes the immediate fiscal cliff but locks itself
into a politically costly, regressive tax base and externally constrained
operating model that becomes hard to unwind.

Evidence that would change the decision: a credible, legally available,
recurring alternative of comparable scale; materially lower pension-cost
trajectory; defensible proof that service cuts can close the gap without
unacceptable public-safety, infrastructure, or basic-service damage; or a
near-certain path to improved state terms before the next budget deadline.

What not to do: do not reject or defer the revenue option while relying on
temporary relief, optimistic negotiations, one-time reserves, or unspecified
cuts as the core fiscal plan. That would be a non-plan unless backed by a
specific recurring substitute.

Expected downside if wrong: residents bear a higher and regressive tax burden,
local elected officials lose flexibility, some constrained spending choices may
crowd out other priorities, and public trust may erode if leadership cannot show
that the new revenue directly prevented worse service and pension outcomes.
