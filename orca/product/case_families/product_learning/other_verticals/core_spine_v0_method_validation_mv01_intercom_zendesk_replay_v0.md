## 5. First-Order Evidence Units (EvidenceUnit)

### MV01-FE1 - Intercom made Fin a buyer-visible AI-agent wedge before cutoff

Intercom publicly framed Fin as a GPT-4 customer-service bot that could answer from existing support content, reduce support volume, require minimal setup, use safeguards, cite source articles, and hand off to humans. The near-official PR similarly positioned Fin as customer-service-ready, accurate, zero-training, and support-volume-reducing.

- Signal integrity: real first-order vendor/product signal, strong for competitor narrative visibility, weak for independent buyer pull.
- Signal use classification: `competitor narrative and product-claim evidence`.
- Action ceiling effect: supports `watch`, `probe`, `test`, and `reposition`; does not by itself justify `move`.

### MV01-FE2 - Zendesk already had pre-cutoff AI-agent and copilot positioning

Zendesk's April 2024 first-order material announced AI agents, agent copilot, workforce engagement, QA/WFM additions, generative AI features, HIPAA-enabled AI use, and admin/productivity updates. The owner correction explicitly treats April 2024 Zendesk AI-agent material as pre-cutoff first-order evidence.

- Signal integrity: first-order Zendesk product and positioning evidence, with vendor metrics treated as claims rather than independent demand proof.
- Signal use classification: `incumbent response-capability evidence`.
- Action ceiling effect: caps the need for a panic move; strengthens a `defend/reposition/test` response around credible existing capability.

Public operator/community discussion before cutoff admired Intercom's AI-first positioning but raised cost, per-resolution, and affordability objections. The signal is audience-relevant but contaminated by small-team bias, competitor participation, and limited evidence of costly switching.

### MV01-SE2 - Zendesk AI discussion exposed add-on, knowledge-base, and fit concerns

Public Zendesk community discussion before cutoff questioned whether AI features were free, whether add-on pricing made sense, how AI handled help-center knowledge boundaries, and whether cheaper or better marketplace alternatives could fill gaps.

- Signal integrity: medium-low; real practitioner concerns but fragmented and partly vendor-promotional.
- Movement: `Reframe`.
- Action ceiling effect: suggests Zendesk should defend on native workflow fit, knowledge controls, admin governance, and transparent measurement rather than only matching Fin's headline.

### MV01-SE3 - Intercom community pricing discussion supplied earlier measurement friction

The Intercom community thread showed pre-cutoff customer pushback about per-resolution pricing, cost caps, no-response resolution attribution, volume scaling, and whether Fin made financial sense relative to other support options.

- Signal integrity: medium; first-party hosted but includes customer and Intercom-team exchanges.
- Movement: `Downgrade pressure against a pure Fin-matching move`.
- Action ceiling effect: strengthens the case for `narrow`, `test`, and `reposition`, while weakening a broad `move` based on Fin's launch narrative.

## 7. At-Cutoff Memo

- Decision frame: Zendesk should respond to Intercom Fin as a credible buyer-visible AI-agent pressure signal, but not treat Fin's vendor narrative as proof that Zendesk buyers were ready to switch or that a broad direct match was a reliable bet.
- Evidence basis: first-order Intercom and Zendesk sources prove public AI-agent positioning on both sides before the cutoff. Second-order operator/community sources prove salience, price sensitivity, and workflow/measurement objections, but not sustained Zendesk-versus-Fin switching pressure.
- Signal Integrity summary: first-order evidence is visible and relevant but vendor-shaped. Second-order evidence is independent enough to sharpen the response angle, but too fragmented and promotional in places to prove costly behavior.
- Signal Use Classification summary: `competitive narrative pressure with practitioner-friction reframing`.
- First-order versus second-order movement: first-order supports `test/reposition`; second-order causes `Reframe`, not `Upgrade`, by shifting the response from feature matching to measurement, pricing, knowledge-control, admin-governance, and enterprise workflow fit.
- Decision Strength: `MODERATE_LOW`.
- Action Ceiling: `TEST_REPOSITION_DEFEND`; `MOVE` is not proven.
- Recommendation: `test` a bounded competitive-response program and `reposition/defend` Zendesk AI around enterprise service workflow, measurement transparency, knowledge governance, and pricing clarity. Keep direct Fin-matching claims narrow and evidence-gated.
- Counterevidence: Intercom's own buyer-visible narrative was strong; public discussion admired the AI-first positioning; AI-agent category momentum was visible before cutoff.
- Alternative explanations: public pressure may reflect category hype, competitor marketing, SMB pricing sensitivity, or third-party vendor promotion rather than Zendesk enterprise buyer loss.
- Uncertainty: no private win/loss, pipeline, renewal, churn, or usage evidence; no reliable independent buyer panel; limited public enterprise-administrator evidence before cutoff.
- Kill criteria: downgrade any move if second-order evidence remains mostly small-team or vendor-promotional, if pricing objections dominate, or if no independent switching/evaluation evidence appears.
- Update triggers: upgrade only if independent pre-cutoff-visible evidence shows repeated Zendesk-versus-Fin evaluations, migration consideration, budget movement, workflow change, or renewal pressure with stakes.
- Boundary note: the at-cutoff memo uses no post-window outcome material.

## 9. Post-Window Outcome Comparison

Post-window Zendesk sources show that Zendesk expanded the AI-agent response surface after the cutoff: an October 2024 release introduced AI-powered agents across channels, an enhanced agent copilot, voice AI, and an agent builder; a March 2025 release reframed the product around a Resolution Platform with AI agents, knowledge graph, governance/control, measurement, and outcome-based pricing; OpenAI's 2025 Zendesk page described agentic service agents focused on resolution; and Zendesk's 2026 comparison page positioned Zendesk directly against Intercom on AI agents, governance, reporting, omnichannel maturity, and pricing clarity.

Comparison to the sealed at-cutoff memo:

- What Orca would have recommended at cutoff: `test`, `reposition`, and `defend` around enterprise service workflow, measurement transparency, knowledge governance, and pricing clarity; no reliable-bet `move`.
- What later happened: Zendesk later leaned hard into AI-agent, resolution, governance, measurement, and competitive positioning surfaces.
- Calibration: the recommendation was useful on response dimensions and avoided false causal overclaiming, but conservative relative to the later breadth of Zendesk's AI-agent expansion.
- Method reading: second-order evidence did not prove buyer pull, but it correctly sharpened the response ceiling and the dimensions that later mattered. The later outcome should not be used to retroactively raise the at-cutoff action ceiling above the evidence available before the seal.

## 10. Calibration Label And Method Lesson

- Calibration label: `USEFUL_CONSERVATIVE_REFRAME`.
- Method lesson: For competitor narrative pressure cases, cleaned second-order public evidence can improve the response shape without proving enough costly buyer behavior for `move`. The method should reward reframing when second-order evidence identifies the right decision dimensions, while preserving a hard ceiling against vendor-claim-driven `Move` or `Commit`.
